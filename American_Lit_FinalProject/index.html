How do Bechdel’s formal choices support her reference to the Myth of Icarus? What else do Bechdel’s formal choices reveal?

At the start of the novel, Bechdel describes her relationship with her father as being analogous to the myth of Icarus.

The myth of Icarus tells the story of Daedalus, an extremely accomplished craftsman and his son. Icarus plummets to his death because he does not heed his father’s warnings and flies too close to the sun.

In this panel, young Bechdel and her father are depicted in a state of freefall– arms and legs are flailing.

Contrasting colors in clothing

Bechdel and Bruce’s contrast in the color of their clothing points toward the yin yang.

This observation is also supported by Bruce’s posture in the second panel, which is suggestive of the convolution/twist in the yin yang.

Yin and Yang is a “Chinese/Daoist philosophical concept that describes how opposite or contrary forces may actually be complementary/interdependent in the natural world, and how they may give rise to each other as they interrelate”

Their contrasting characteristics and clashing personalities are a keystone for Bechdel’s development. She spotlights the key differences between herself and her father on Page 15 by dividing the page into four rectangular panels of equal size.

The differences are further emphasized by Bechdel’s creative diction and the separate illustrations included in each panel.

Looking closer at the panels themselves give us further insight. Alison expresses her rugged masculinity.

Her stance portrays her as protecting the family and watching over the her father– a masculine feature.

Bruce, on the other hand, is reclining and reading. His position and the type of book he is reading illustrates his feminine traits and foreshadows conflicts regarding sexuality.

What else do Bechdel’s formal choices reveal?

Looking back at the first few pages, Even in this rare moment of father-daughter activity, Bruce still obsessed over small details of the house. Her father’s eyes are on the rug and his face is expressionless.

Bechdel uses visual imagery (facial expression) and dialogue to reveal her father’s terse and taciturn, but also authoritarian nature. Bechdel herself is framed as seeking her father’s affection and attention.

Monomaniacal - mental illness, limited in expression to one idea or area of thought.

Bruce’s obsession of restoring the family’s home created a facade of a Christian family that disguised his homosexuality.

Here, Bechdel exits the scene. Her facial expression shows that this isn’t the first time her father has done something like this.

Bruce’s gaze is distant. It seems as though he is looking through Bechdel– distracted.

On pages 220-221, Bechdel divides the page into uniform square panels of the same size. This subconsciously forces the reader to read the dialogue at a slower pace.

*In these pages, Bechdel places herself in the foreground, while her father is slightly further away and elevated.

Bruce’s facial expression transitions to an alert state starting from panel three. His eyebrows are furrowed and his eyes widen.

It is also important to note the abundance of the color black in these two pages, as Bechdel’s panels are largely blue and white. Black is associated with power, mystery, seriousness, and authority.

All of these factors combined with the angle and positioning of Bruce and Bechdel places Bruce on a pedestal and shows that he is making himself vulnerable.

Odysseus and Telemachus’ reunion reunion is the result of years of careful planning, searching, and hardship. It culminates in a loud, chaotic and violent slaughter of the suitors.

Bechdel’s comparison of their reunion using specific literary references show that the two are not particularly close.

Stephen and Bloom refer to the two main characters in Ulysses, Bruce’s favorite book of all time.

7 Eccles street was a row house in Dublin, Ireland. This location is the home of protagonist Bloom in Ulysses.

Right: Row house- single family homes built side by side.

The plot of Ulysses is about Stephen’s search for a father and Bloom’s search for a son– similar to Telemachus’ quest to find Odysseus in The Odyssey. Bloom embarks on this search because he feels a strong urge to establish his identity and heritage through parenthood. Stephen’s search involves finding a father figure who will support and guide Stephen himself to be the ideal father.

Despite their differences, BOTH STEPHEN AND BLOOM HAVE A NEED FOR EACH OTHER, JUST LIKE BRUCE AND BECHDEL. This goes back to the concept of yin yang, which proposes that opposing forces may actually be interdependent and complementary

Bruce and Bechdel’s homosexuality is common ground between the two. In their final moments together, Bruce steps outside of the closet and reveals his secret to Bechdel. She bonds with her father over this moment and even adopts a supportive, paternal attitude throughout the entire interaction: they complement/complete each other.

Upon further analysis, Bechdel’s combination of artistic choices and literary references give readers a much deeper understanding of the relationship dynamics between herself and her father. Despite being polar opposites, giving them the appearance of a dysfunctional relationship, Bruce and Bechdel personalities are actually complementary and interrelated like the yin yang.
